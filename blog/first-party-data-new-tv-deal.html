First-Party Data Is the New TV Deal

Bill Riesner
February 17, 2026 ¬∑ 8 min read

Global sports media rights just broke $62 billion for the first time last year. That's billion with a B. NFL's ESPN deal alone is worth over $2 billion annually. The NBA just locked in $76 billion over 11 years.

But here's the thing: those deals are negotiated by leagues, not teams. If you're an athletic director or partnership lead, you see a fraction of that revenue, split across dozens of teams. Revenue sharing means parity, not competitive advantage.

The revenue you CAN control? First-party fan data. And most teams have almost none.

The Gold Mine You're Sitting On

The global sports industry is projected to grow from $507 billion to $630 billion by 2028. The global fanbase is expanding from 3.2 billion to 4 billion by 2033. That's 800 million new fans in less than a decade.

The question isn't whether fan engagement will grow. It's whether you'll know who those fans are.

Right now, most teams can't answer basic questions:

  • Who bought tickets to the last game?
  • Which fans engaged with which sponsors?
  • What's the lifetime value of a season ticket holder vs. a single-game buyer?
  • How do you reach fans in the off-season?

Third-party data used to fill those gaps. Not anymore.

The Cookie Jar Is Empty

Third-party cookies are dying. Safari and Firefox already block them. Chrome rolled out IP Protection in Q3 2025. The infrastructure that powered digital advertising for two decades is disappearing.

For sports teams, this isn't just a marketing problem. It's a revenue problem.

Sponsors don't pay for impressions anymore. They pay for proof. Proof that fans engaged. Proof that their activation worked. Proof of ROI.

Without first-party data, you're guessing. And sponsors don't write seven-figure checks based on guesses.

The industry knows what's coming. Multi-billion dollar companies are building entire products around first-party fan data. The question is whether individual teams are ready.

What First-Party Data Actually Unlocks

First-party data isn't just email addresses in a spreadsheet. It's the foundation for everything modern fan engagement promises:

Personalized experiences. Not "Dear Fan" emails. Not one-size-fits-all promotions. Relevant offers based on actual behavior.

Sponsor ROI proof. Which fans tapped which sponsor activations? Who opted into communications? Who converted? That's the data that renews partnerships.

Revenue optimization. Right offer, right fan, right time. Season ticket upsells to high-engagement fans. Single-game promotions to casual attendees. Merch bundles to collectors.

Year-round engagement. Game day is 10-15% of the calendar. The other 85% is where fan databases pay off.

Look at El Paso Locomotive FC. They built a fan database from scratch using zero-party data collection. Fans sourced through data campaigns represent approximately 20% of all ticket sales. Of those fans, 57% chose season tickets.

That's not a marketing metric. That's revenue.

One USL campaign captured over 6,000 fan entries. Nearly 40% opted into ongoing sponsor communications. That's not just engagement. That's proof a sponsor can take to their board.

The Collection Problem

Here's where most teams get stuck.

Apps? 37% of downloads are abandoned immediately. Even if fans download your app, how often do they open it after the season ends?

QR codes? Fans scan, maybe fill out a form, and disappear. You get one data point, if you're lucky.

Email gates? Fans give you their junk email address to get the thing they actually want, then never open another message.

The problem isn't that fans won't share data. It's that most collection methods create friction or require something fans don't want to give up.

This is where NFC changes the equation.

Premium team apparel with embedded NFC turns your merch into a persistent data collection point. A fan taps their phone to a jacket, hoodie, or scarf. No app download. No form. No login wall. Just tap and go.

Every tap is a data point:

  • Who (device ID, profile if registered)
  • When (timestamp)
  • Where (location data)
  • What (which piece of apparel, which sponsor activation)

That apparel lives in their closet. They wear it to games. They wear it to watch parties. They wear it to the grocery store. Every time they tap it, you learn something.

And because it's zero-friction, tap rates are high. Fans do it because it's easy, not because you bribed them.

The Teams Getting It Right (and Wrong)

Some teams are investing in customer data platforms (CDPs). They're building data clean rooms for sponsor collaboration. They're treating fan data as infrastructure, not a nice-to-have.

Others are still relying on third-party ticket reseller data they don't own. Social media follower counts that don't translate to revenue. Or worse ‚Äî nothing at all.

Women's sports revenue is up 300% since 2021. New leagues are launching. Fanbases are exploding globally. The data gap will only widen.

The teams that build robust first-party databases now will control their revenue destiny for the next decade. The teams that don't will keep splitting league media deals and hoping for the best.

The Question You Need to Answer

TV deals are great. Sponsorships are great. Ticket sales are great.

But none of them matter if you don't know who your fans are.

First-party data is the foundation. It powers personalization. It proves sponsor ROI. It turns casual fans into season ticket holders. It gives you leverage in partnership negotiations because you can show, not tell.

The question isn't whether first-party data matters. It's whether you're collecting it.

And if you're not, what's the plan?
