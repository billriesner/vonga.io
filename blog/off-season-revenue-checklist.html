The Off-Season Revenue Checklist: 15 Things to Do Before August

Bill Riesner
March 23, 2026 · 7 min read
TL;DR

The offseason is when revenue teams build their competitive advantage. This checklist covers 15 specific actions across five categories: Sponsor Relations, Ticket Revenue, Merchandise, Fan Database, and Infrastructure. Some take an hour. Some take a month. All of them set you up for a stronger revenue year. Print it out. Pin it to your wall. Check things off.

The offseason is when programs build their competitive advantage. While everyone else waits for the season to start, the best revenue teams are already working on next year's numbers.

Here are 15 things to get done before August.

Sponsor Relations

1 Schedule Every Renewal Conversation Now

Don't wait until the contract is about to expire. By then, you're negotiating from weakness.

Action Pull your sponsor list. Any contract expiring in the next 12 months gets a "check-in" call scheduled for the next 30 days. Frame it as relationship maintenance, not a sales call.
2 hours for scheduling, 30 minutes per call

2 Build Your Sponsor Report Templates

Every sponsor should get a mid-season and end-of-season report showing what their investment delivered. But no one builds these templates until they're scrambling to produce one.

Action Create a 5-slide template now: activation summary, audience reached, engagement metrics, renewal recommendation, next-season opportunities. Fill in the specifics later.
3 hours

3 Document Your Wins and Losses

What worked last season? What didn't? The details fade fast. By August, you won't remember which activations drove engagement and which flopped.

Action Write a one-page "sponsor activation retrospective" for each major partner. What you did, what performed, what you'd change. Store it where the whole team can find it.
30 minutes per sponsor

4 Identify Three New Prospect Categories

Same prospect pool means the same results. The offseason is when you expand your target list.

Action Pick three categories you haven't pursued seriously: local healthcare systems, regional banks, technology companies, beverage distributors, insurance providers. Build a list of 10 prospects per category.
4 hours of research

Ticket Revenue

5 Clean Your Season Ticket Database

How many email addresses in your database are invalid? How many phone numbers are disconnected? How many "season ticket holders" from 2019 are still marked active?

Action Export your database. Run emails through a verification tool (NeverBounce, ZeroBounce). Remove bounced addresses. Flag phone numbers that haven't engaged in 18 or more months.
4 to 6 hours (or hire a freelancer for $100)

6 Survey Last Season's Non-Renewers

The people who didn't renew know exactly why they left. Most will tell you if you ask.

Action Send a 3-question survey to last season's lapsed ticket holders. Questions: (1) What was the main reason you didn't renew? (2) What would bring you back? (3) Would you consider a partial plan? Offer a small incentive for completing it: $10 concession credit or early access to single-game tickets.
2 hours to build and send, 2 hours to analyze responses

7 Design Your Early-Bird Campaign

The best ticket revenue comes in the offseason, before games even start. An early-bird campaign with genuine scarcity (limited inventory, deadline pricing) converts better than any in-season push.

Action Decide your early-bird offer. Map the email sequence (announcement, reminder, last chance). Set the launch date. Build the landing page.
6 to 8 hours

Merchandise

8 Audit Your Top 20 SKUs

Most merch programs follow an 80/20 rule: 20% of products drive 80% of revenue. Do you know which 20%?

Action Pull your sales data. Rank by units sold and by margin. Identify your top 20 SKUs. These are the products that deserve better placement, more inventory, and marketing budget.
2 hours

9 Clear Dead Inventory

That 2022 bowl game T-shirt? It's not selling. The longer it sits, the more it costs in storage and opportunity cost.

Action Identify merchandise that hasn't moved in 12 or more months. Run a clearance sale at cost. Donate the rest and take the tax deduction. Use the shelf space for products that actually sell.
4 hours

10 Negotiate Your Supplier Terms

Most programs auto-renew supplier contracts without renegotiating. Margins matter.

Action Review your merch supplier agreements. Are minimum orders still appropriate? Can you get better terms on best-sellers? Is there a volume discount you're not using? One conversation can save thousands.
2 to 3 hours

Fan Database

11 Merge Your Data Sources

Your ticketing system, your email platform, your point-of-sale system, your social accounts: they all have fan data. But do they talk to each other?

Action Export fan records from every system. Identify overlap by email address. Build a master list with all touchpoints consolidated. Yes, this is annoying. Do it anyway.
8 to 12 hours (one-time project)

12 Segment Your Email List

"Send to all" is lazy marketing. Different fans need different messages.

Action Create at least five segments: current season ticket holders, lapsed season ticket holders, single-game buyers, donors, email-only (never purchased). Build one targeted campaign for each segment.
4 hours

13 Reactivate Dormant Fans

Fans who haven't engaged in 18 or more months aren't gone: they're sleeping. A "we miss you" campaign costs almost nothing and often delivers surprising results.

Action Identify fans who haven't opened an email or bought anything in 18 or more months. Send a reactivation sequence: personal note from the AD, exclusive offer, final chance. If they don't engage, move them to a suppression list.
3 hours

Infrastructure

14 Audit Your Website Analytics

Is your tracking set up correctly? Can you see where ticket buyers come from? Do you know which pages convert and which leak visitors?

Action Log into Google Analytics (or whatever you use). Check that tracking is firing on all key pages. Set up goal tracking for ticket purchases, donation forms, and newsletter signups if you haven't already.
2 to 4 hours

15 Document Your Revenue Processes

New staff shouldn't have to reinvent the wheel. The offseason is when you turn tribal knowledge into written playbooks.

Action Pick your three most important revenue processes: sponsor fulfillment, ticket sales campaign, merch ordering. Write a step-by-step doc for each. Include who does what, when, and how.
2 hours per process

The Checklist

Print this out. Pin it to your wall. Check things off.

Sponsor Relations

  • Schedule all renewal conversations
  • Build sponsor report templates
  • Document wins and losses
  • Identify three new prospect categories

Ticket Revenue

  • Clean season ticket database
  • Survey non-renewers
  • Design early-bird campaign

Merchandise

  • Audit top 20 SKUs
  • Clear dead inventory
  • Renegotiate supplier terms

Fan Database

  • Merge data sources
  • Segment email list
  • Reactivate dormant fans

Infrastructure

  • Audit website analytics
  • Document revenue processes

The One Metric That Matters

Track this: How much revenue did you close before the first game?

Pre-season revenue (sponsor renewals, early-bird tickets, merchandise pre-orders) is the best predictor of a strong revenue year. The work you do now determines that number.

Check items off this list. Build the foundation. Don't wait for the season to start.
