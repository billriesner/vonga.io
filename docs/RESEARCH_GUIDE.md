# Research Guide: Verified Data for Website Claims

**Purpose:** Find credible, verifiable data to support quantitative claims on vonga.io  
**Target Audience:** Sports team operators (B2B buyers who will scrutinize claims)  
**Standard:** Data must be citeable, recent (2020+), and relevant to sports merch/fan engagement

---

## Claims Needing Verification

### 1. Revenue Concentration Claim
**Current (unverified):** "85% of annual merch revenue at 1-2 events"  
**What we need:** Data on when/where sports teams generate merch revenue

### 2. Fan Engagement Frequency
**Current (unverified):** "100+ days a year"  
**What we need:** Data on how often fans engage with teams

### 3. Revenue Per Fan / Lifetime Value
**Current (unverified):** "$50 vs $150+ lifetime"  
**What we need:** Average transaction values and repeat purchase data

### 4. Post-Purchase Engagement Gap
**Current (qualitative):** "Merch ends at checkout"  
**What we need:** Data on post-purchase engagement rates in sports merch

---

## Research Protocol

### Priority Order
1. **Industry Reports** (highest credibility)
2. **Academic Research** (peer-reviewed, rigorous)
3. **Survey Data** (if methodology is transparent)
4. **Case Studies** (real teams, verifiable results)
5. **Expert Interviews** (quotable, attributable)

### Citation Standard
**Acceptable:** "According to [Source], [Stat]"  
**Not acceptable:** Vague "studies show" or "research indicates"

## Using the Data You Find

### Citation Format

**Strong citation (use this):**
> "According to a 2024 Sports Business Journal study, 73% of college athletic departments report that championship event merchandise accounts for the majority of their annual apparel revenue."

**Weak citation (avoid):**
> "Studies show that most merch revenue happens at events."

### Data Quality Checklist

Ask yourself:
- [ ] Is the source credible and recognized?
- [ ] Is the data recent (published 2020 or later)?
- [ ] Is the methodology transparent?
- [ ] Is the sample relevant to our audience?
- [ ] Can I cite this without embarrassment?
- [ ] Would a sophisticated buyer trust this source?

### When You Can't Find Exact Data

**Option 1: Use Related Data**
> "While specific data on minor league sports is limited, the broader sports merchandise industry shows significant revenue concentration around key events. According to [Source], professional teams see a [X%] spike in merchandise sales during playoff periods."

**Option 2: Use Directional Language**
> "Athletic directors consistently report that the bulk of merchandise revenue happens at a concentrated set of key events throughout the season."

**Option 3: Commission Your Own Research**
> Conduct 15-20 interviews with target customers, document findings, publish as "Vonga 2026 Sports Team Merchandising Survey"

## Red Flags to Avoid

❌ **Don't use:**
- Unsourced infographics
- Marketing material from competitors
- "According to research" (without naming source)
- Data older than 5 years
- Non-transparent methodologies
- Sample sizes under 100 (for quantitative claims)

✅ **Do use:**
- Peer-reviewed research
- Recognized industry publications
- Transparent methodologies
- Recent data (2020+)
- Relevant samples (sports teams, not general retail)
